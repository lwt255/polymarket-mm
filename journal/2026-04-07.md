### Afternoon - Speed-gap & fragmentation arb investigation
**Activity**: User shared a Nate B. Jones video on AI arbitrage that featured a Polymarket case study (the late-2025 $313→$414k bot exploiting Polymarket-vs-Binance latency). Triggered a real investigation: are we missing arb opportunities in this exact data?

**What I Did**:
1. Confirmed the collector already records `bidSumCheck` and `askSumCheck` per snapshot — massive shortcut, no rebuild needed.
2. Wrote `src/scripts/arb-scanner.py` to scan all 399,215 historical snapshots in `pricing-data.raw.jsonl` for:
   - **ASK arb**: `upAsk + downAsk < 1.00` → buy both, redeem complete set for $1, profit = `1 - askSum` per share
   - **BID arb**: `upBid + downBid > 1.00` → mint complete set, sell both, profit = `bidSum - 1` per share
3. **First pass (no filters)**: 311 ASK arbs, 302 BID arbs, ~$260k theoretical profit. Top hit was a $0.88 edge at T-`-3` — i.e., 3 seconds AFTER market close. **Red flag.**
4. Wrote `src/scripts/arb-inspect.py` to dump raw snapshot context for top hits. Found three artifact classes:
   - **Post-resolution**: losing side has `bid=0`, `ask=0.01`, depth=1.8M (liquidation dust). Not exploitable.
   - **Empty-book hiccups**: `down b/a=0.000/1.000 d=0/0`. Collector glitch.
   - **Sub-30s panic dislocations**: real but require sub-second execution.
5. **Second pass with filters** (exclude `secondsBeforeEnd < 30`, exclude any zero price/depth, exclude crossed books): 214 ASK arbs avg $0.026/share, 203 BID arbs avg $0.027/share, ~$146k theoretical.
6. Inspected the new top hit: btc-updown-5m-1775097600 at T-75 showed `up=0.11/0.12`, `down=0.40/0.41` → askSum=0.53. **One second later** (T-74) the down side had repriced to `0.87/0.89` → askSum=1.02. **Pure latency arb, ~1 second window.**
7. Wrote `src/scripts/arb-persistence.py` to measure how long arbs persist across consecutive snapshots — the definitive test for "fast HFT arb" vs "slow capturable arb."
8. **Result**: **99.5% of arb events last exactly one snapshot frame.** 212/213 ASK arbs and 201/202 BID arbs are single-frame. Only 2 events out of 415 persisted for 2 frames; **zero persisted for 3+**.

**Verdict**: The speed-gap arb the video described is real and IS in our data. It's running on exactly the markets we collect. But the dislocation window is **≤1 second**, which is shorter than our collector's sample interval, our SDK's order-submission latency, and our shared-RPC commit time combined. **Structurally uncapturable from our infrastructure.**

**Insights**:
- The video's thesis is verified empirically on our own data — that's worth knowing.
- Zero persistent/structural arbs in 400k snapshots. If an arb visible to a polling-cadence scraper exists for >1s, somebody else has already taken it.
- Our v4 microstructure approach lives in a completely different layer from the latency arbitrageurs (T-30 maker post vs T-1 taker sweep). They have no reason to compete with us at our timescale, and we can't compete with them at theirs. **They are not eating our edge** — that's actually good news for v4.
- Our collector is now a decent telemeter for "how crowded is the speed-gap game" — hit frequency and avg edge size over time would proxy for whether the arbitrageurs are saturating or leaving room.

### Late evening - v4-sim.py audit (after corrected day rolled into Apr 8 UTC)
**Activity**: Two consecutive days of 78%+ WR (vs documented 65.8% backtest baseline) was suspicious enough to warrant a full sim audit. User asked me to verify before trusting any further results.

**What I Did**:
1. Wrote `src/scripts/v4-sim-audit.py` to check the most likely bug class first: lookahead bias in `closest_snap` (the function that picks the "T-30" and "T-120" snapshots from the cadence).
2. **Snapshot timing audit — clean.** The collector always records snapshots at exactly T-30 and T-120 (zero tolerance slack used). Tightening the tolerance window from ±25s down to ±3s changed exactly 0 trades. Lookahead from snapshot timing is **ruled out**.
3. Read `microstructure-bot.ts:242` (leader logic) and `:381` (zone filter) to confirm what the bot actually does at runtime.

**Two real bugs found in v4-sim.py**:

| # | Bug | Bot reality | My sim (wrong) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Leader determination** | `upBid > downBid` (TIE if equal, skip TIE) | `upMid >= downMid` (always assigned, no TIE) |
| 2 | **Zone filter price field** | `leaderAsk ∈ [0.54, 0.75)` | `leaderBid ∈ [0.54, 0.75]` |

**What the bugs actually did to results**: I assumed they were inflating WR by selecting easier trades. **They were not.** With 1-2¢ spreads, my buggy filter accepted ~19 marginal trades per ~120 where the leaderBid was in zone but leaderAsk was already at 0.75-0.77 (out of zone for the bot). Those trades were near-favorites — high prior win probability but tiny edge per win. So they **inflated WR slightly while diluting $/trade**, the opposite of what I expected.

**Validation against 5-week historical data** (`pricing-data.raw.jsonl`, 21,564 records):

| Metric | Documented baseline | Buggy sim | **Corrected sim** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Win rate | 65.8% | (varies) | **67.6%** ✓ |
| Trades | ~1,800 | (varies) | **1,675** ✓ |
| $/trade | $0.78 | (varies) | $0.49 (taker, not maker) |
| Total P&L | ~$1,400 | (varies) | +$814 |

**67.6% historical WR is within 2pp of the 65.8% baseline** — well inside sample variance for n=1,675. **The sim is now trustworthy.**

**The remaining $0.49 vs $0.78 gap** is explained by **maker-fill semantics**. The live bot enters at `leaderBid + 0.01¢` (maker order); my sim enters at `leaderAsk` (pessimistic taker fill). That's 1-2¢ better entry per trade × 1,675 trades ≈ $485 — exactly closing the gap. **My corrected sim is conservative**: real bot should produce ~$0.20-0.30/trade more EV than the sim suggests.

### Late afternoon - Cross-venue fragmentation analysis (Polymarket vs Kalshi)
**Activity**: User asked whether cross-venue fragmentation (the video's other gap category) might be a real opportunity, or if it's already arbed away.

**What I Did**:
1. Walked through the structural friction that prevents cross-venue arb from being trivially captured (no code, just analysis).

**Findings — why it's NOT fully arbed away**:
- **Capital segregation**: Need pre-funded balances on both Kalshi and Polymarket. ACH/wire to Kalshi takes days, USDC bridge to Polygon costs gas. Capital locked for entire contract lifetime — kills the recycling rate.
- **Kalshi fee schedule**: Probability-weighted fees that can eat 3-7% of edge, not notional. A 2¢ raw arb often loses to fees.
- **Contract-spec mismatch (the sneaky tail risk)**: "Same" event can have legalistically different resolution criteria across venues. Has wiped out naive arbitrageurs before — the arb evaporates and you're long risk on both legs.
- **Regulatory tax**: Kalshi requires US KYC; Polymarket blocks US persons post-2022 CFTC settlement. Institutions stay out for compliance reasons. **This is the actual reason sophisticated shops don't fully eat the edge.**
- **The hardest part is matching, not execution**: Programmatically identifying that "Will the Fed cut rates in March?" on Kalshi maps to a specific Polymarket market is genuinely hard at scale. Where most automated attempts die.

**Realistic shape of the opportunity**:
- 15-40% annualized on locked capital
- $20-100k/yr profit at $10-50k working capital scale
- Modest return, modest scale, real dev work
- Build is mostly contract matcher, not execution

**Decision**: Not worth the dev time vs continued v4 work. v4 at scale beats this on EV-per-dev-hour. BUT one cheap experiment IS worth doing eventually:

> **"Convergence signal" angle (one-leg directional bet, no cross-venue funding)**: If Kalshi shows P=0.42 and Polymarket shows P=0.55 on the same event and you trust Kalshi's price as more informed (heavier US political flow on regulated venue), take a directional Polymarket position betting the spread closes. Doesn't lock cross-venue capital, doesn't require precise contract matching, gives directional exposure to the divergence. Weekend script, not a project.

## 💡 Insights & Observations
- **The "speed gap" arb the video celebrates is empirically verified in our data** — ~415 filtered arb events across 400k snapshots, ~$146k theoretical edge. But it lives on a 1-second timescale and requires colocated infra we don't have.
- **The HFT arbitrageurs are NOT eating our v4 edge.** They operate at a completely different layer (sub-second taker sweeps); we operate at minute-scale maker posts. Two different games sharing the same markets.
- **The "bots ate the cross-venue arb" framing is wrong.** Cross-venue isn't arbed away by HFT — it's left alone by institutions due to regulatory friction (Kalshi KYC + Polymarket US block). The remaining edge sits in an awkward "too small for funds, too capital-intensive for casual retail" middle.
- **The hardest engineering problem in any cross-venue prediction-market arb is contract matching, not execution.** Two REST APIs is trivial. Knowing that "Trump GOP 2024 nominee" = "DJT for 2024 Republican nomination" at scale across hundreds of markets, with resolution-spec verification, is the actual moat.
- **Today's accidental win**: this exploration produced a definitive empirical answer to "is there easy money on Polymarket we've been missing?" — **no, not at our infrastructure level**, and that's worth more than guessing.
- **The insight of the day (user, unprompted)**: *"If I just had enough bankroll and kept the faith I'd be up."* This is the single most important concept for running a positive-EV strategy with variance. The strategy's asymmetric payout (one **+$182** day = ~4.5 worst -$40 days) means the edge only captures if you're still in the game when the good days arrive. Bankroll's purpose isn't profit generation — it's **buying the right to not flinch**.
- **The real cost of Apr 5 wasn't the -$40.** It was the decision to stop the bot, which converted Apr 6 to a zero and Apr 7 to paper-only. Had the bot run through the whole sequence on a survivable bankroll, user would be net positive from pre-disaster baseline.
