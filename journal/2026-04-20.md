## Design discussion: target-based stopping

User observation on the low-WR wins: "Like I'd just stop trading for the day after those 2 big wins. That may not be the right way to think about it but those wins make up for everything."

### The tension

**Pure-math view**: each trade is independent +EV. Stopping after wins doesn't change the expected value of subsequent trades. Missing trades = missing edge.

**Human-utility view**:
1. Utility is concave. +$90 → +$150 (good) feels smaller than +$90 → +$40 (loss) even though +$40 is still positive.
2. Variance drag matters at small bankrolls. Day σ ~$50 means good-day followed-by-bad-day is the expected pattern. Locking in outsized positive variance trades expected return for smoother equity curve.

This is **target-based stopping** — theoretically suboptimal for pure +EV maximization, but it's a legitimate insurance-type trade. Widely used in discretionary trading practice.

### What the bot currently does

Existing stops:
- `--max-loss 25` — halts on $25 drawdown
- `--trail 75` — halts on $75 drop from peak (so after +$135 peak, stop ratchets up to +$60)
- `--sweep-step 20` — alerts (not halts) every $20 above starting balance

No hard daily-win cap. Trailing stop provides some natural "lock in gains" behavior but loose ($75 trail on $5 stakes = 15 trades worth of losses).

### Decision

Not adding a daily-win cap for dry-run — we need to collect realistic variance data to validate the strategy before imposing stops that change its observed distribution.

**When low-WR flips to live**: revisit. Add `--daily-win-cap <N>` flag that halts after +$N session profit and resumes next candle cycle. Value of N TBD based on 48-72h dry-run observation. If daily σ truly is ~$50 at $5 stakes, a $50 cap would let you keep ~1σ gains and miss less than 25% of +EV.

**The user's instinct flags a real design consideration** — preserving good days has real psychological value even if it costs a bit of expected return. Worth implementing as an option when going live, not as a default.
