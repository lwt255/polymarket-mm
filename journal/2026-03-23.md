## Session 3: Collector Throughput & Trust Fixes

### Throughput Math Was Wrong

Validated the collector capacity and found the old `~96/hour` estimate was incorrect.

Actual ceiling with the current market universe:
- **4 x 5-minute markets** (BTC, ETH, SOL, XRP) = `4 * 12 = 48/hour`
- **4 x 15-minute markets** (BTC, ETH, SOL, XRP) = `4 * 4 = 16/hour`
- **True max = 64 markets/hour**, not 96

The old number implicitly treated all 8 markets as if they refreshed every 5 minutes. That was a math error, not an exchange/API blocker.

### Under-Sampling Bug Fixed

The collector loop was also missing eligible windows.

Root cause:
- It processed the **soonest-ending batch**
- Then slept to the next 5-minute boundary
- In practice this meant we were often collecting closer to **~32/hour** instead of the real **64/hour** ceiling

Fixed `src/scripts/pricing-collector.ts` so it no longer idles between alternating market groups. The live collector was restarted after the patch and is now capturing the current batch stream under the new logic.

### Garbage Data Now Quarantined

Found a real bad-data mode in the collector:
- A recent 5-minute batch produced **11 snapshots of empty books** (`UP=0 / DOWN=0 / ask=1`) for all 4 markets
- Those records were previously being written into `pricing-data.jsonl`
- Simulated trades were also being generated from non-tradable snapshots, which polluted running stats

Fixes added:
- Empty-book snapshots are now **called out explicitly in the log**
- Simulated trades now **skip non-tradable snapshots**
- Records with too few snapshots, all-empty books, or no tradable snapshots are now written to **`pricing-data.rejected.jsonl`** instead of the main dataset

### Historical Dataset Cleaned

Applied the same acceptance rules retroactively to the current dataset.

Results:
- `pricing-data.jsonl`: **2,113 -> 2,101 accepted**
- `pricing-data.rejected.jsonl`: **12 quarantined records**
- Backup saved as: `pricing-data.pre-clean-20260323-1458.jsonl`

Rejected rows included:
- 4 startup records with only **2 snapshots**
- 4 all-empty-book records from the bad 5-minute batch
- 4 older no-tradable / incomplete records from early collector runs

### Strategy Implication

This materially changes how much confidence we should place in prior collector-derived win rates and filters.

We were not looking at a fully sampled stream, and a small amount of garbage data had also made it into the main dataset. The previous numbers are **not invalid**, but they are now clearly **provisional**.

The key uncertainty is directional:
- Full 64/hour collection could make the strategy look **better**
- Or it could make it look **worse**

We do not know yet. We need fresh, clean runtime under the corrected collector before treating any filter edge as settled.

### Key Insights

1. **The collector trust issue had two layers** — the headline throughput assumption was wrong, and the actual loop was also under-collecting below the true ceiling.
2. **Data quality needs quarantine, not silent tolerance** — bad rows must be separated from the primary dataset or every downstream stat becomes suspect.
3. **Current filter conclusions are now conditional** — until we accumulate enough clean data from the fixed collector, every edge estimate should be treated as subject to revision.

### Liquidity Quality Is Now Measured Directly

Key additions:

- Snapshot schedule was densified late in the market from `T-75` down to `T-5`

### Empty Liquidity Does Not Mean Free Money

Clarified what the empty-book pattern likely means operationally:
- Usually there were **no actionable resting quotes** at the snapshots we captured
- That does **not** automatically mean Polymarket was broken
- It also does **not** automatically mean a missing market maker is leaving easy spread income on the table

Interpretation:
- For a taker strategy, no liquidity means **the signal was not tradable**
- For a maker strategy, no liquidity may indicate opportunity, but it can also mean other participants stepped away because the adverse-selection risk was too high

This matters because earlier filter stats may have been measuring some combination of edge and survivorship from only the tradable windows we happened to catch.
